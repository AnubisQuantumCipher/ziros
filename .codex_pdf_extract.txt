1619Ð2026: A Continuous Extraction Machine
The financial condition of Black America is not an accident. It is the result of a continuous,
documented, four-century extraction machine that has operated in different forms but never
stopped running.
Phase 1: Chattel Slavery (1619Ð1865).
 For 246 years, Black labor was the primary capital
asset of the American economy. Enslaved people were collateral for loans, the basis of
insurance products, and the engine of cotton and tobacco exports. On the eve of the Civil
War, the white-to-Black per-capita wealth ratio was approximately 60 to 1.
Phase 2: Reconstruction Sabotage (1865Ð1877).
 The FreedmanÕs Savings Bank,
established by Congress in 1865, took in approximately 
#57 million from Black depositors
(roughly 
#1.5 billion today). White financiers on the bankÕs board redirected deposits into
speculative loans. The Panic of 1873 collapsed the bank. Over 61,000 Black families lost
their savings. The federal government never made depositors whole. This was the template:
invite Black participation, extract the value, leave the community holding the loss.
Phase 3: Jim Crow Wealth Destruction (1877Ð1964).
 Every time Black communities built
independent economic power, the response was destruction. The Tulsa Race Massacre of
1921 destroyed the Greenwood District Ñ 35 city blocks, over 1,200 homes, hundreds of
businesses, an estimated 
#610 million in accumulated wealth-building capacity destroyed in
two days. Before the massacre, the dollar reportedly circulated 36 to 100 times within

Greenwood before leaving. The community never recovered. Simultaneously, Black farmers
Ñ who had acquired 15 million acres by 1910 Ñ were systematically stripped of their land
through USDA loan discrimination, tax sale manipulation, and heirsÕ property partition
abuse. By 1997, Black-owned farmland had fallen to less than one-sixth of the 1910 figure.
Phase 4: Redlining and Housing Exclusion (1934Ðpresent).
 The FHA refused to insure
mortgages in Black neighborhoods. White families used FHA-backed loans to buy suburban
homes that appreciated massively. Black families were locked out. The homeownership gap
Ñ 25 percentage points in 1976 Ñ has widened to 30 percentage points as of 2022 (45%
Black vs. 75% white).
Phase 5: Predatory Financial Extraction (1990sÐpresent).
 Payday lenders charge
average APRs of 400% and concentrate 8 times more heavily in Black neighborhoods. In
California alone, payday lenders drain approximately 
#250 million annually from Black and
Latino communities. Wells Fargo loan officers internally called subprime loans marketed to
Black communities Òghetto loans.Ó During the 2008 crisis, median Black household wealth
dropped from 
#19,200 to

White household median wealth:
